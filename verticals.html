Industries we know from the inside

Not generalist ERP — deep, repeat experience in the operations that are hardest to configure well.

Food & beverage

Perishability, batch accountability, and recall-readiness make F&B one of the least forgiving verticals to implement wrong. Standard inventory logic doesn't hold up against shelf life, catch-weight, and formula-driven production — and when it doesn't, the failure shows up as a recall you can't trace fast enough, not just a reporting error.

We configure Business Central around how F&B operations actually move product: variable-weight receiving, expiry-driven picking, and formulas that account for yield loss and by-products — not just a straight bill of materials.

Where we go deep
  • Lot & batch traceability — full genealogy from raw material lot to finished goods, so a recall can be scoped to the exact affected batches instead of an entire product line
  • Catch-weight inventory — both fixed and variable weight items, with pricing and inventory value calculated off actual received weight, not nominal
  • Recipe & formula management — including by-products, co-products, and yield variance tracking against theoretical output
  • FEFO-driven picking — first-expired-first-out logic enforced at the warehouse level, not left to manual judgment
  • Allergen and certification tracking — flagged at the item level and inherited through production
  • Shelf-life-driven inventory valuation — aging stock flagged before it becomes a write-off

Automotive DMS

Dealer networks run on systems that were never designed to talk to each other — a dealer management system for sales and service, OEM portals for warranty and incentives, and finance running separately in BC or another ERP. The work is less about configuring Business Central in isolation and more about making it the connective layer that reconciles all three without manual re-entry.

Where we go deep
  • VIN-level inventory tracking — new and used vehicle stock tracked individually, not as generic inventory items, through the full ownership lifecycle
  • Parts & service integration — parts inventory tied to service work orders, including core charge tracking and returns
  • Warranty claim management — claims tracked from submission through OEM reimbursement, with aging visibility on outstanding claims
  • OEM-facing reporting — sales, inventory, and service data structured to match OEM reporting formats without manual reformatting each cycle
  • F&I integration — finance and insurance product attach data flowing into deal financials automatically
  • Service scheduling & bay utilization — visibility into technician capacity against booked appointments

Manufacturing

Production data that reaches finance days late is a planning problem in disguise — by the time a cost overrun shows up in the ledger, the batch that caused it is long finished. The goal is tying shop-floor activity directly into financial reporting, so decisions get made on what's happening on the floor right now, not what happened last week.

Where we go deep
  • BOM & routing accuracy — multi-level bills of materials with routing steps that reflect actual production sequence, including subcontracted operations
  • Capacity planning — both finite and infinite loading models, matched to whether your bottleneck is a machine, a shift, or a skilled operator
  • WIP tracking — work-in-progress valued and visible at each production stage, not just at order completion
  • Standard vs. actual costing — variance analysis that flags where actual production cost is drifting from standard before it compounds across a full run
  • Quality management integration — inspection points and non-conformance tracking built into the production flow, not bolted on separately
  • Subcontracting workflows — outsourced operations tracked with the same cost and lead-time visibility as in-house steps
